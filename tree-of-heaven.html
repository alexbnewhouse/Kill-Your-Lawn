Kill the Tree of Heaven.

Ailanthus altissima — a fast-spreading invasive that chokes out natives, cracks foundations, and rolls out the welcome mat for the spotted lanternfly. Here's how to spot it and actually kill it.

Why it has to go

A tree that fights the whole neighborhood

  • Poisons the soil around it — its roots release ailanthone, a chemical that suppresses native seedlings nearby
  • Spreads by aggressive root suckers, sending up clones yards away from the parent and forming dense thickets
  • One of the fastest-growing trees in North America, with weak, brittle wood that drops limbs
  • Roots heave sidewalks and invade foundations, sewer lines, and septic systems
  • Female trees rain down 300,000+ winged seeds a year that blow into every open lot
  • It's the preferred host of the spotted lanternfly — remove it and you remove their nursery
How to actually kill it

Kill the roots first, then cut

  1. Time it: mid-July to October. The tree is pumping sugars down to its roots — herbicide rides along. Spring treatment fails.
  2. Treat it standing. Seedlings < 3 ft: foliar spray (triclopyr or glyphosate). Stems < 6 in: basal bark — triclopyr in oil around the bottom 12–18″. Bigger: hack & squirt concentrated herbicide into angled cuts ringing the trunk.
  3. Wait 30+ days, then cut and remove the dead tree. If you must cut-stump, paint herbicide on within 30 minutes.
  4. Watch for 2–3 years. Re-treat every sucker and seedling. Bag female seed material — don't compost it.
The #1 mistake

Never just cut it down

Cut an untreated Tree of Heaven and the roots panic — a single stump can throw up dozens of suckers across your yard. You don't get rid of it; you multiply it. Herbicide while it's standing, in late summer, is what kills the root system.

How to identify it

The leaves

Huge compound leaves 1–4 ft long, with 11–41 smooth-edged leaflets. Leaves alternate up the twig — not opposite.

Glandular teeth

The tell: 1–2 bumps ("glands") at the base of each otherwise smooth leaflet. Native look-alikes don't have them.

The bark

Smooth, pale gray-brown, lightly mottled — often described as the skin of a cantaloupe. Stays smooth even on big trunks.

The smell

Crush a leaf or snap a twig: a rank, offensive odor people compare to cat urine or rotten peanut butter.

Don't confuse it with natives. Sumac has toothed, serrated leaflets and fuzzy red stems; black walnut has dark, diamond-ridged bark and a chambered twig pith; ash has opposite leaves. Tree of Heaven's combo — smooth leaflet edges, basal glands, and that smell — is unique.

Plant these instead
Native & Adapted Shade Trees
  • Bur oak (Quercus macrocarpa)
  • Western hackberry (Celtis occidentalis)
  • Kentucky coffeetree (Gymnocladus dioicus)
  • Narrowleaf cottonwood (Populus angustifolia)
Small Trees & Large Shrubs
  • Gambel oak (Quercus gambelii)
  • Serviceberry (Amelanchier alnifolia)
  • Chokecherry (Prunus virginiana)
  • American plum (Prunus americana)
Quick Structure & Habitat
  • Three-leaf sumac (Rhus trilobata)
  • Golden currant (Ribes aureum)
  • Rabbitbrush (Ericameria nauseosa)
  • Wax currant (Ribes cereum)
Know the rules & get help
Colorado Law

List C Noxious Weed

Tree of Heaven is on Colorado's noxious weed list. The state recommends control; counties may have local programs. See the Colorado Dept. of Agriculture (ag.colorado.gov) noxious weed pages.

Watch Pest

Spotted Lanternfly

Not yet established in Colorado (as of 2025), but it threatens grapes, peaches, and hops. If you ever see it or its egg masses, report to the CDA. Removing Tree of Heaven removes its top host.

Get Help

Do It Right

CSU Extension (extension.colostate.edu) and the Boulder County weed program have guides. Always follow the herbicide label — it's the law — and hire a licensed applicator for large trees.
