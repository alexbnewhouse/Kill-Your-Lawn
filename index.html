The Case

Your lawn is an ecological dead zone.

Turf grass covers roughly 40 million acres in the US — more than any single crop we grow. It demands water, pesticides, and fossil fuels to produce exactly nothing: no food, no shelter, no real roots. The shortgrass prairie that covered this part of Colorado for millennia? Reduced to scattered fragments. And you're standing on a piece of where it used to be. Replacing your lawn with native prairie is one of the most direct, measurable things an individual can do about climate change — and unlike most climate actions, this one also saves you money and makes your yard more interesting.

Water

Lawns consume roughly 30–60% of residential water in the West. Deep-rooted native plants, once established, need little or no supplemental irrigation. Their roots reach several feet down — often 5–10 feet or more, depending on the species — to find their own.

Insects & Birds

As Doug Tallamy documents in Nature's Best Hope, 96% of terrestrial bird species require insects to raise their young. Native plants support the caterpillars and beetles that feed them. Exotic turf grass supports almost none.

Soil

Lawn soil is typically compacted and biologically dead. Native plant roots rebuild soil structure, feed mycorrhizal networks, and sequester carbon. You go from a maintenance liability to a living system that improves itself every year.

Place

Lafayette sits right in the transition between shortgrass prairie and mountain foothills. Native plantings reconnect your yard to that landscape, creating stepping stones of habitat through an otherwise fragmented suburban grid. You're not starting from scratch. You're filling in a gap.

The invisible argument: what's happening underground

Your lawn's root system is 4 inches deep. That's it. Everything the lawn does — carbon storage, water management, drought survival, fire recovery — is limited to that thin, fragile layer. Native prairie roots go 5–15 feet deep, and that depth changes everything.

Prairie banks its carbon underground. Forests store most of theirs above ground in trunks and branches — vulnerable to fire, disease, and drought. Prairie stores it in deep root biomass and stable soil organic matter, where it stays locked for decades.

On land that was historically grassland — like the entire Front Range — prairie restoration sequesters more soil carbon than planting trees, and holds it more durably. Trees are great where trees belong. This was never forest.

Convert a typical 5,000 sq ft lawn and you get both sides of the ledger: soil that sequesters carbon year after year once established, and an end to the ongoing emissions from mowing, fertilizing, and irrigating turf. The gains build slowly, but they compound for decades.

Prairie is built to come back. Shortgrass prairie evolved with fire and drought over millions of years. Deep root crowns survive both and push new growth within weeks. Bluegrass has no such mechanism — when it dies, it's dead. As Colorado gets hotter and drier, which root system do you want?

Every square foot of native prairie is a small piece of climate infrastructure — storing carbon, managing stormwater, and surviving the conditions that are already arriving. A lawn does none of this. People say individual action can't move the needle on climate. But most of those people are imagining you turning off a light switch. This is different. You're converting a net carbon source (mowed, fertilized, irrigated turf) into a net carbon sink — on land you already own, with results that compound every year. That's not symbolic. That's measurable.

Systems Thinking

Think in systems.

You've heard the line: individual action doesn't matter at scale. And if you think linearly, that's true. But ecology doesn't work linearly. It works in feedback loops — and once you see them, you can't unsee them.

The habitat flywheel: each element feeds the next. Your yard starts the spin.

The flywheel

Native plants feed insects. Insects feed birds. Birds scatter seeds. Seeds colonize bare ground nearby. More habitat, more insects, more birds — the thing accelerates on its own. But the part that's easy to miss: each yard that joins the network doesn't just add to the total habitat. It multiplies the connections between fragments. That's not a metaphor — that's literally how network math works. Metcalfe's law, applied to coneflowers.

The mosaic model

Okay — this is what convinced me. Wildlife corridors don't have to be continuous strips of preserved land. Ecologists call it the mosaic model: a patchwork of small, connected habitat fragments can be ecologically more valuable than the same total area clumped in one preserve. Your 1,000 square feet of native plants? Not a rounding error. It's a node. And nodes have outsized power when they bridge a gap between fragments that are otherwise isolated. That's how you build a corridor through suburbia: one yard at a time, closing the dead zones between the green ones.

The same math applies to carbon. One yard converting from lawn to prairie sequesters a ton or two of CO2 per year. A block of ten does ten times that, obviously — but it also creates a contiguous root network with better water infiltration, deeper soil biology, and more stable long-term carbon storage than ten isolated patches. Connected prairie soil is more than the sum of its parts.

The social feedback loop

There's a social dimension too. You plant natives. Your neighbor sees monarchs for the first time in years. They ask what that purple thing is. A couple of them plant their own. Now there's more habitat, more visible wildlife, more curious neighbors. The proof generates itself — you don't have to argue anyone into it. This is how behavior actually spreads: not through guilt, not through policy, but through visible results that make the next person's decision feel obvious. Every converted yard is a walking advertisement for the one next to it.

The feeling that nothing you do matters — "the problem is too big, why bother" — comes from thinking about this linearly. It assumes your contribution is a drop in a bucket. But you're not filling a bucket. You're joining a network. And in a network, every new node makes all the existing nodes more valuable. Your yard isn't a drop. We've heard it all

Your objections, answered honestly.

We had every one of these doubts before we started. Here are the honest answers — not the sales pitch.

This is the most common concern — and the most understandable. The difference between a native garden and an abandoned lot is design and intention. Edges matter enormously: a clean mown border, a defined path, a few boulders, or a simple sign signals to passersby that this is deliberate, not neglect. Thomas Rainer and Claudia West's Planting in a Post-Wild World is essentially a manual for making ecologically-driven gardens look unmistakably intentional.

It also takes time. Year one looks sparse. Year two looks promising. Year three looks like something. The neighbors who laughed often become the ones asking what that purple flower is. We've seen this happen on our own block.

And honestly: Kentucky bluegrass in July in Colorado looks like beige carpet. There's a low baseline here.

The opposite is closer to true. Conventional lawns are actually prime pest habitat, and the data on this for Colorado is striking.

Japanese beetles. Popillia japonica was first detected in Colorado in the mid-1990s and has spread rapidly across the Front Range ever since. The reason: irrigated Kentucky bluegrass lawns are their perfect breeding ground. Female beetles lay eggs in moist, well-watered turf; the larvae (white grubs) spend months underground eating grass roots before emerging as adults. Over-irrigated suburban lawns are essentially beetle nurseries. Reducing or eliminating lawn irrigation directly reduces Japanese beetle populations — this is now standard advice from CSU Extension. A dry, native grass planting offers dramatically less attractive breeding habitat.

Grubs, skunks, and raccoons. Skunks and raccoons tear up lawns specifically to eat those grubs. If you've had your lawn dug up overnight, thank the grub population your irrigation schedule created. Remove the grubs' habitat and you remove the animals digging for them.

Mosquitoes breed in standing water, not in plants. A rain garden or bioswale planted with natives can actually reduce mosquito habitat by draining standing water faster. Native plantings also support predatory insects (dragonflies, parasitic wasps, ground beetles) that keep pest populations in check naturally.

Ticks in Colorado are much less of an issue than in the humid East, and they're not associated with native gardens specifically. Keep mown edges at entry points if you're concerned.

Colorado law has your back. HB 21-1229 (2021) and the stronger SB 23-178 (2023) explicitly prohibit HOAs from banning drought-tolerant or native plant landscaping. An HOA can still set reasonable standards around maintenance appearance, but they cannot require you to maintain traditional turf grass or prohibit xeriscape and native plantings outright. HOAs of single-family homes must now offer at least three pre-approved water-wise landscaping designs.

If you get pushback, cite SB 23-178 directly. You can also engage your HOA proactively: bring photos of finished native gardens, show them the water savings data, and frame it as an amenity, not a rebellion. We have a sample letter in the Legal section below.

This is a genuinely important question in Colorado, and the answer is nuanced. A well-designed native garden can actually be more fire-resistant than a conventional lawn in several ways:

  • Native forbs (broadleaf wildflowers) have high water content and burn poorly compared to dried grass
  • Native gardens typically include gravel mulch, decomposed granite, and stone — all non-combustible
  • Blue grama and buffalo grass, our native shortgrass prairie species, grow short and sparse — very different from the tall, continuous fuel beds of unmown ornamental grasses
  • A dead, drought-stressed bluegrass lawn in August is actually quite flammable

The key is zone design: keep the area immediately adjacent to your house (within 5 feet) clear of combustible plants. Use rock mulch near foundations. CSU Extension has excellent fire-wise native plant guidance specific to the Front Range.

Worth unpacking what "weed" means here. Kentucky bluegrass, the standard Colorado lawn grass, is native to Europe and Asia. It spreads aggressively by rhizome into natural areas, outcompeting native grasses. It is, by the ecological definition, an invasive weed. The same is true of most conventional lawn species.

True native plants evolved in community with each other and with local insects and fungi. They don't typically behave invasively in their home region. Some do spread by rhizome (Maximilian sunflower, for example) or self-seed freely (coneflowers, gaillardia), but this is easily managed by mowing at the edges or pulling volunteers.

If you're worried about a specific plant spreading, ask us. We're happy to choose species that stay in their lane.

Totally valid, and compatible with going native. Nobody is saying eliminate every blade of grass. The approach most families take is to keep a small functional turf area (back yard, side yard, wherever play happens) and convert the front lawn and less-used areas to natives. A 400 sq ft patch of buffalo grass handles most backyard play needs, uses a fraction of the water of a full bluegrass lawn, and can be mown short.

Kids who grow up with a native garden also get something a mown lawn can't offer: bugs to observe, flowers to pick, caterpillars to find, birds to watch. The yard becomes genuinely interesting. We'd argue that's worth more than a flat green surface.

The evidence actually points the other way, especially in the West. Water costs are real money in Colorado home calculations now, and a mature native garden that requires zero irrigation is a legit selling point. Front Range buyers increasingly expect water-wise landscaping — it's not the novelty it was ten years ago.

Studies of xeriscape conversions in the arid West — including University of Nevada research on residential xeriscape — have found neutral-to-positive effects on property value, with no measurable reduction and often a modest premium, largely because buyers factor in lower ongoing maintenance and water costs.

Also: a well-designed native garden photographs beautifully for listings, especially in spring and fall when lawns look like beige carpet and natives are peaking.

A lawn is constant low-grade work: mowing weekly, watering on a schedule, fertilizing, reseeding bare patches, treating for grubs. It never stops. A native garden is front-loaded: there's real effort in year one to establish plants and control weeds while things are getting roots down. Years two and three you're troubleshooting and filling in gaps. By year four, you're mostly just watching.

The long-term math is unambiguous. No mowing. No irrigation schedule. No fertilizer. A few hours a year of editing: removing unwanted volunteers, cutting back dead stalks in spring (which provide overwintering habitat for solitary bees). That's it.

The transition is the hard part. Once you're through it, the garden largely runs itself.

Kentucky bluegrass is native to the steppes of Europe and Central Asia. It was imported in the 1600s and has since spread across most of North America, outcompeting native grasses in disturbed areas. In Colorado, it escapes lawn edges into riparian corridors and grassland remnants where it displaces native species.

More importantly: it supports almost no native insects. Native oaks (Quercus) support over 500 species of caterpillar. Native goldenrod supports 115 species. Kentucky bluegrass supports functionally none. It didn't co-evolve with anything here. A biological desert that happens to look green for about three months of the year.

Doug Tallamy's research frames this starkly: every square foot of exotic monoculture is a square foot that produces no food for the insects that feed birds, that feed larger predators. The cumulative effect of 40 million acres of lawn is a wildlife catastrophe in slow motion.

Getting Started

How to kill it (and what comes next)

No single right method. It depends on your timeline, budget, and how entrenched your bluegrass is. Here are the main approaches, roughly from laziest to most labor-intensive.

01

Solarization

Best: June–August

Cover your lawn with clear plastic sheeting for 6–8 weeks during the hottest part of summer. Trapped heat kills grass, roots, and weed seeds in the top few inches of soil. Free-ish, effective, and low-effort. You just wait.

  • Mow low first, water thoroughly
  • Lay 2–4 mil clear plastic, weight the edges
  • Leave it until grass is thoroughly cooked (yellowed and dead)
  • Plant directly into the mulch layer or till lightly
02

Sheet Mulching (Lasagna Method)

Year-round

Smother grass with overlapping layers of cardboard (remove tape and staples), then bury it under 4–6 inches of wood chip mulch. Grass dies as the cardboard decomposes. This is the method that builds soil simultaneously.

  • Water the lawn before laying cardboard
  • Overlap cardboard edges by at least 6 inches — no gaps
  • Source free wood chips from Chip Drop or local arborists
  • Wait 6–12 months, or plant through the mulch immediately
03

Smothering with Tarps

6–12 months

Black silage tarps block all light and kill everything beneath them. Slower than solarization but effective year-round and reusable. Great for large areas or fall/winter starts.

  • Use woven black poly tarps, not landscape fabric
  • Weight heavily — wind is the enemy
  • Check edges monthly for sneaky grass escapes
04

Hand Removal + Sod Cutting

Labor intensive, immediate

The old-fashioned way: rent a sod cutter, strip it out, compost or flip the sod. You get instant results and can plant immediately. Best for smaller areas or people who want control.

  • Roto-till only as a last resort — it spreads rhizomes
  • Flipping sod upside down and leaving it in place is a valid lazy option
  • Amend soil after removal before planting
05

After Killing: Seed or Plant?

The next step

Both work, but they require different timing. Seeding is cheaper and better for large areas; transplants establish faster and give instant structure. Many people do both: transplants as anchors, seed to fill in.

  • Best seeding windows: early spring (April) or late fall (October–November) for cold stratification
  • Broadcast seed, rake lightly, and keep moist until germination
  • Expect 2–3 years before a planted meadow looks intentional — hold steady
Lower the Barrier

You don't have to do it all at once.

The biggest mistake is treating this as all-or-nothing. You don't need a master plan. Pick one area, learn what works in your dirt, and expand when you're ready.

A

The Hell Strip

Easiest entry point

The strip of ground between the sidewalk and the street (often called the tree lawn or hell strip) is typically the most neglected and hardest-to-water part of your yard. It's also the most visible to neighbors and passersby, which makes it the highest-impact place to start.

Note: the city owns this strip, but in Lafayette you're responsible for maintaining it. Native plantings are generally allowed — check with the city if you want confirmation.

Good starter plants: Blue grama, prairie coneflower, buffalo grass, scarlet globemallow, Rocky Mountain penstemon

B

One Corner or Edge Bed

Low stakes, high visibility

Convert a single bed along a fence line, property edge, or the corner of your lawn. Define it with a clean edge (mown grass, gravel, or stone) and plant 5–10 species you're genuinely curious about. This is your test plot and your proof of concept.

A 4×12 ft bed costs under $100 in seed and transplants, takes a weekend to establish, and can look genuinely beautiful by year two.

C

The Pollinator Patch

Maximum wow, minimum work

Plant a dense 6×6 patch of the highest-impact native bloomers: purple prairie clover, wild bergamot, prairie coneflower, and blanket flower. Mulch it well. By midsummer you'll have more bees and butterflies than you've seen in years, and neighbors will start asking questions.

This is the starter project that sells you on the rest. Once you see what a few square feet of the right plants can do, the logic for scaling up becomes obvious.

D

Lawn on a Diet

Gradual transition

Don't touch the back yard. Just convert 25% of the front lawn this year. Next year, another 25%. By year four, you've made the full transition with no single daunting project, low startup cost, and a chance to learn what works in your specific soil and microclimate before committing the whole yard.

This is especially good if you have an HOA or a skeptical partner. The incremental change is harder to object to.

E

Native Lawn Replacement

Best of both worlds

If you genuinely want a mowable, walkable surface but not Kentucky bluegrass, replace it with blue grama and buffalo grass, the actual native grasses of the Colorado shortgrass prairie. They form a dense, low turf, go dormant (tan) in summer, green up beautifully in spring, and need essentially no supplemental water after establishment.

You still get a lawn. You just get the right one.

For the Lawn Insistent

You can keep a lawn. Just use the right grass.

If you genuinely need a mowable surface (kids, dogs, HOA optics), you don't have to use Kentucky bluegrass. The shortgrass prairie that once covered this exact ground was, in fact, a low-growing lawn. Here's what it was made of, and what it can do for you.

First: identify what you're actually dealing with

Most Colorado yards have one of four grasses, often in a mix. Knowing which one matters because they differ in how hard they are to kill, how deep their roots go, and how much water they demand.

Kentucky Bluegrass

Poa pratensis — Eurasia
  • ID: Fine, boat-shaped leaf tip; deep blue-green color; spreads by underground rhizomes to form dense sod
  • Roots: 2–6 inches — cannot reach water without irrigation
  • Water demand: Extremely high — needs 1–1.5" per week in summer
  • Japanese beetles: Primary host grass in Colorado — female beetles strongly prefer moist bluegrass for egg-laying
  • Kill difficulty: Hard. Rhizomes survive most surface treatments; solarization or repeated smothering required

Tall Fescue

Festuca arundinacea — Eurasia
Less bad — not good
  • ID: Coarse, wide leaf blades; clumpy growth; lighter green than bluegrass; no rhizomes — grows in bunches
  • Roots: 2–3 feet — meaningfully deeper than bluegrass (4") or ryegrass (6"), and this difference is real. But context: native shortgrass prairie grasses root at 5–7 feet. Fescue reaches roughly 15–25% of what evolved here.
  • Water demand: Moderate — a genuine step up from bluegrass. In a typical Colorado summer, fescue needs ~0.5–0.75" per week versus bluegrass's 1–1.5". That's a real reduction. But "significantly less thirsty than bluegrass" is not the same as "drought tolerant" — established native grasses need essentially zero supplemental irrigation.
  • Japanese beetles: Moderate host — less preferred than bluegrass but still supports egg-laying when irrigated
  • Native insects: Supports essentially zero. Fescue evolved in Eurasia. It co-evolved with Eurasian insects, not Colorado ones. Better roots do not improve ecological function.
  • Kill difficulty: Moderate. No rhizomes, so sheet mulching and solarization work well. Clumps can be dug out
A step up from bluegrass on water. Still at zero on ecology.

Perennial Ryegrass

Lolium perenne — Europe
  • ID: Shiny underside of leaf blade; fine texture; germinates very fast (7–10 days); often mixed with bluegrass seed
  • Roots: 4–6 inches — extremely shallow; the shallowest of common lawn grasses
  • Water demand: Very high. Wilts and browns first in drought
  • Kill difficulty: Easy. Shallow roots die quickly under any smothering method. Often the first to go in a conversion
  • Note: Doesn't overwinter reliably on its own in CO — usually a component of a seed mix rather than a pure stand

Bermuda Grass

Cynodon dactylon — Africa/Asia
  • ID: Gray-green, fine-textured; spreads aggressively by both rhizomes AND stolons (above-ground runners); goes straw-tan in fall and stays that way until late spring
  • Roots: 6+ feet — surprisingly deep for how much water it demands
  • Water demand: Moderate when established; higher when actively growing. Becoming more common in southern CO as temperatures rise
  • Kill difficulty: Very hard. One of the most persistent lawn grasses — rhizomes and stolons regenerate from small fragments. May require multiple solarization cycles or repeated smothering over 1–2 seasons
  • Note: If you have Bermuda, expect the conversion to take longer. Don't skip steps.

The native lawn grasses, in detail

Blue grama (Bouteloua gracilis)

Best all-around native lawn
4–6"Mown height
5 ftRoot depth
Near zeroIrrigation (est.)
Warm seasonGrowth type

The defining grass of the Colorado shortgrass prairie. Forms a fine-textured, dense turf that can be mown or left to its natural height of 6–10 inches. Goes dormant (tan/bronze) in winter and green in spring. The distinctive eyelash-shaped seed heads are beautiful left standing. Extremely drought tolerant once established — in an established prairie, it receives no supplemental water.

Establishment: Slow from seed — expect 2 years to fill in. Germination is best with soil temps above 60°F (late May–June in Lafayette). Can be overseeded into dying bluegrass. Plugs establish faster. Does not tolerate heavy shade.

Mowing: Optional. Can be mown to 3–4" as a low-maintenance turf, or left at natural height. No mowing needed after October. Cut dead growth in early spring before green-up.

Buffalo grass (Bouteloua dactyloides)

Lowest-maintenance native turf
3–5"Mown height
~6 ftRoot depth
Near zeroIrrigation (est.)
Warm seasonGrowth type

Buffalo grass forms a dense, fine-textured sod that spreads by stolons (above-ground runners) to fill in over time. Historically the dominant grass of the Great Plains, it is the grass that fed the bison herds. Dioecious — male and female plants are separate. Stays very low (3–5") without mowing, making it the most truly no-mow native lawn option.

Goes dormant and gray-tan in summer heat and winter cold; greens up reliably in spring. The dormancy is often alarming to new growers — it's not dead, it's resting.

Establishment: Slow and requires patience. Female-only cultivars (Prestige, Bison, Cody) are available as plugs or sod and establish more quickly than seed. Plant plugs 12–18" apart; they'll knit together within 2–3 seasons.

Best for: Low-traffic areas where the no-mow aesthetic is acceptable. Less wear-tolerant than blue grama under heavy foot traffic.

Mixing blue grama and buffalo grass in a 60/40 or 50/50 ratio mimics the actual shortgrass prairie community and produces a more resilient, varied turf than either alone. Blue grama handles wear and variation better; buffalo grass fills in bare spots via stolons. Together they cover the full range of Front Range conditions.

Pre-mixed seed blends are available from Wildflower Farm, Native American Seed, and Prairie Moon Nursery. Seed in late May or early June for best germination. Water until established (first season), then largely leave it alone.

Converting to native turf: the practical steps

  1. 1
    Kill the bluegrass thoroughly. Don't skip this. Kentucky bluegrass rhizomes are tenacious — any survivor will be back. Solarize in summer or sheet mulch over winter. One incomplete kill means starting over.
  2. 2
    Amend soil minimally. Native grasses evolved in lean, alkaline, well-drained soils — exactly what most Colorado yards have. Don't add compost or fertilizer before seeding; it encourages weeds. Rake the soil surface lightly.
  3. 3
    Seed or plug in late spring. Blue grama and buffalo grass are warm-season grasses — they need soil temps above 60°F to germinate. In Lafayette that means mid-May through June. Broadcast seed at 1–2 lbs per 1,000 sq ft; rake in lightly; keep moist for 2–3 weeks.
  4. 4
    Manage weeds aggressively in year one. The hardest part. Hand-pull or spot-treat anything that isn't your grass. Native grasses are slow to establish and will lose to weeds if you don't intervene. This is temporary — by year two the turf will suppress most weeds on its own.
  5. 5
    Water year one only. Supplemental irrigation during establishment (first growing season) gives your grasses a head start. After that, let them be. If you water established native turf like bluegrass, you'll actually push it back into dormancy stress and invite weeds.
  6. 6
    Mow once in early spring, then let it go. Cut dead growth to 2–3" in March before green-up. After that: nothing. Or mow once mid-summer to 4" if you want it tidier. The rest of the year, the grass manages itself.
What to Grow

Natives for the Front Range shortgrass prairie

These plants evolved here. They're adapted to our alkaline clay, our dry summers, our late frosts, and our intense sun. Once established, they handle themselves — and they handle everything else, too.

Grasses & Sedges

  • Blue grama (Bouteloua gracilis) The signature grass of the Colorado shortgrass prairie. Stunning eyelash seed heads, drought-proof, and provides critical habitat for prairie birds. Can be used as a low-mow lawn alternative.
  • Buffalo grass (Bouteloua dactyloides) Dense, low-growing, and truly native turf. Goes dormant and tan in summer, green in spring. Zero irrigation once established. Slow to spread from seed but worth it.
  • Side-oats grama (Bouteloua curtipendula) Taller than blue grama, with striking oat-like seeds hanging from one side of the stem. The official state grass of Texas, but native throughout the Front Range. Beautiful in fall.
  • Inland saltgrass (Distichlis spicata) Extremely tough, tolerant of alkaline and even salty soils. Spreads by rhizome to form dense mats. Good for problem areas.
  • Prairie dropseed (Sporobolus heterolepis) Elegant mounding grass with a faint fragrance. Excellent wildlife value, beautiful texture. Slow to establish but long-lived.

Wildflowers — Forbs

  • Purple prairie clover (Dalea purpurea) Magenta-pink flower spikes from June–August. Fixes nitrogen. One of the best native plants for bees and butterflies on the Front Range.
  • Rocky Mountain penstemon (Penstemon strictus) Vivid violet-blue tubes from June into August. A magnet for native bees — bumble, mason, and digger bees — with the occasional hummingbird visit. Long-lived, extremely drought tolerant, and absolutely stunning en masse.
  • Prairie coneflower (Ratibida columnifera) Cheerful yellow-and-maroon cones that bloom for months. Self-seeds prolifically. Easy from seed, beloved by goldfinches who eat the heads.
  • Maximilian sunflower (Helianthus maximiliani) Tall, late-season gold (August–October). Spreads by rhizome to form colonies. A critical fall fuel source for migrating monarchs and goldfinches.
  • Blanket flower (Gaillardia aristata) Fiery red-and-yellow blooms all summer. One of the easiest natives to establish from seed. Excellent for pollinators.
  • Prairie smoke (Geum triflorum) One of the first to bloom in spring. Feathery pink seed heads look like wisps of smoke. Short, ground-hugging, and distinctive.
  • Wild bergamot (Monarda fistulosa) Lavender-pink flowers with incredible fragrance. Spreads slowly by rhizome. Beloved by native bees and hummingbird moths.
  • Scarlet globemallow (Sphaeralcea coccinea) Brilliant orange-red blooms on drought-hardened plants. Thrives in poor, gravelly, alkaline soils where other plants struggle.
  • Narrowleaf yucca (Yucca glauca) Structural, dramatic, and essential. Its relationship with the yucca moth is one of the most remarkable mutualisms in the plant kingdom.
  • Golden aster (Heterotheca villosa) Cheerful late-season yellow blooms. Very drought tolerant, long-blooming, and excellent for native bees when little else is flowering.

Shrubs & Structure

  • Apache plume (Fallugia paradoxa) White rose-like flowers followed by spectacular feathery pink seed heads. Extremely drought tolerant. Structure year-round. Native to southern Colorado and the Southwest — not local to the Front Range, but a well-adapted xeric shrub here.
  • Rabbitbrush (Ericameria nauseosa) Late-season gold (September–October) when almost nothing else blooms. Critical fall refueling stop for monarchs and countless other insects.
  • Three-leaf sumac (Rhus trilobata) Brilliant fall color — orange, red, burgundy. Drought tolerant, spreads to form thickets. Excellent wildlife value.
  • Leadplant (Amorpha canescens) Deep roots (up to ~15 feet) — a true prairie indicator species. Purple flower spikes with orange anthers in July. Fixes nitrogen.

The Structure Layer

Shrubs and trees, and who comes with them.

Wildflowers feed the pollinators; woody plants build the house. Shrubs and trees are the structure of a yard's food web — nesting sites, winter cover, berries when nothing else is fruiting, and the caterpillar biomass that 96% of backyard birds need to raise their young. Skip the developer's default — Siberian elm, silver maple, Bradford pear — and plant a few natives that actually feed something. Add them, and the guest list changes.

Trees, where there's room

  • Bur oak (Quercus macrocarpa)

    The keystone — built for open savannah, deep-rooted and good for a century. No tree feeds more caterpillars, and caterpillars are what baby birds eat. Plant instead of a Norway maple.

    Hundreds of caterpillars Jays & woodpeckers — acorns Squirrels & deer
  • Gambel oak (Quercus gambelii)

    The foothills' own thicket-forming oak. A wild, layered edge instead of a clipped privacy hedge.

    Caterpillar host Scrub-jays & wild turkey Mule deer & black bears
  • Chokecherry (Prunus virginiana)

    Small tree, enormous value — a caterpillar keystone like the oaks, with spring flowers and summer fruit. Plant instead of a Bradford pear.

    Spring pollinators Swallowtails & hairstreaks Waxwings & robins Black bears
  • Netleaf hackberry (Celtis reticulata)

    Tough, overlooked, and excellent — it shrugs off heat, wind, and lousy alkaline soil while feeding a surprising crowd.

    Hackberry emperor & snout — host Mourning cloak Berry-eating birds
  • Plains cottonwood (Populus deltoides)

    The signature giant of the plains rivers — and a five-star host. Big and genuinely thirsty, so right only with a low, damp spot and real room.

Right tree, right place: this was never forest, so think scattered savannah and creekside copses — not a wall of shade. Pick one or two, give them room, and let them grow into the neighborhood. A single oak outlives all of us, and feeds the block the whole time.

Meet Your Neighbors

These are bees, not wasps.

Everyone on the Front Range has a legitimate grievance with yellowjackets. But the insects that move into a native garden are a completely different story. Colorado is home to roughly 950 native bee species — and almost none of them want anything to do with you, your picnic, or your soda can.

Mining bees (Andrena)

Solitary Ground-nesting Rarely stings

Small, fuzzy, and completely docile. They nest in bare soil — the little volcano-shaped mounds you see in spring are theirs. They're among the first bees active each year and are critical pollinators for early-blooming native plants. No hive to defend means no reason to sting.

Sweat bees (Agapostemon)

Solitary Tiny Virtually stingless

Yes — that iridescent green jewel is a bee. Agapostemon sweat bees are among the most beautiful insects in Colorado and among the most effective pollinators of native wildflowers. Sweat bees as a family got the name from being drawn to perspiration (the brilliant green Agapostemon less so than their smaller cousins), and their sting is so mild most people don't even feel it.

Bumble bees (Bombus)

Social (small colonies) Docile unless grabbed

The big fuzzy ones. Colorado has ~24 native bumble bee species, several now in decline. They nest in abandoned rodent burrows and old bunchgrass clumps — exactly the kind of habitat a native garden provides. They're gentle, slow-moving, and critical for buzz-pollinating plants like tomatoes and peppers.

Mason bees (Osmia)

Solitary Cavity-nesting Non-aggressive

Dark, metallic blue-black bees that nest in hollow stems and beetle holes. A single mason bee pollinates as effectively as 100 honeybees. They're active in early spring when fruit trees bloom. You can support them by leaving dead plant stems standing through winter — that's where they overwinter.

Leafcutter bees (Megachile)

Solitary Carry pollen on belly Very gentle

If you see perfect little circles cut from a leaf, that's a leafcutter — she's using them to wallpaper her nest cells. They carry pollen on their furry bellies instead of their legs, which makes them exceptionally efficient pollinators. Completely non-aggressive.

So what about the yellowjackets?

Yellowjackets and paper wasps are not bees — they're a completely different branch of Hymenoptera. They're attracted to human food (especially protein and sugar), which is why they show up at barbecues. Native bees have zero interest in your hamburger. They want pollen and nectar from flowers — specifically, the native flowers they co-evolved with.

And worth knowing: native plantings draw bees looking for pollen and nectar, not the scavenging yellowjackets that crash your cookout for protein and sugar. A native garden won't manufacture a yellowjacket problem — and the wasps it does host are themselves predators, hunting the caterpillars and flies that would otherwise chew through a garden. They're part of the same working ecosystem, not a sign that something has gone wrong.

Know Your Rights

Colorado law is on your side.

You have way more legal protection for native landscaping than most people realize. Here's what you need to know before your HOA sends a letter — and exactly what to do when they do.

Colorado Law

HB 21-1229 (2021) & SB 23-178 (2023)

Two laws now explicitly limit HOA authority over landscaping. HB 21-1229 (2021) established initial protections; SB 23-178 (2023) significantly strengthened them. Key provisions:

  • HOAs cannot prohibit drought-tolerant or native plant landscaping ("xeriscaping")
  • HOAs cannot require homeowners to maintain traditional turf grass
  • HOAs must allow an option of at least 80% drought-tolerant plantings and cannot prohibit vegetable gardens
  • HOAs of detached single-family homes must offer at least three pre-approved water-wise landscaping designs
  • HOAs can still set reasonable maintenance and aesthetic standards — but they can't mandate grass specifically
  • If your HOA wrongfully prohibits or penalizes xeriscape or native landscaping, SB23-178 lets you take the association to court and recover damages of at least $500 — after you give written notice and a 45-day period to cure. (Separately, an HOA that ignores a written request for its governing documents faces a $50/day penalty up to $500, which is useful if you need those records.)

Both bills passed with bipartisan support. They were a direct response to HOAs fining homeowners for converting lawns during drought years.

City of Lafayette

Local Ordinances and Weed Codes

Lafayette's municipal code includes a "nuisance vegetation" ordinance, but it targets genuinely noxious and invasive weeds, not native plants. Key points:

  • State-listed noxious weeds (Canada thistle, puncturevine, bindweed, etc.) must be controlled regardless of your landscaping choice
  • Native plants are not classified as noxious weeds under Colorado or Lafayette municipal code
  • Maintaining a defined, mown edge at the sidewalk and property line significantly reduces the chance of complaints or code enforcement inquiries
  • A simple sign explaining the native garden (or a QR code to this site) is a highly effective way to preempt neighbor concerns

If you receive a notice from the city, contact Lafayette Public Works to clarify that your plantings are native species, not noxious weeds. This typically resolves the issue.

Template

HOA Response Letter

If your HOA sends a violation notice, use this as a starting point:

Dear [HOA Board / Property Manager],

I am writing in response to the landscaping notice dated [date]. The plantings in question are drought-tolerant native species selected for water conservation and habitat value, consistent with xeriscape principles.

I want to call your attention to Colorado House Bill 21-1229 (2021) and Senate Bill 23-178 (2023), which prohibit HOAs from banning or penalizing drought-tolerant or native plant landscaping. Under these laws, the HOA may set reasonable maintenance and aesthetic standards but may not require the maintenance of traditional turf grass or prohibit xeriscape landscaping outright.

I am committed to maintaining the planting in a tidy, well-edged condition. I am happy to meet with the board to discuss the planting plan and address any specific aesthetic concerns within the bounds of what the law allows.

I look forward to resolving this cooperatively.

Sincerely,
[Your name]

Not an organization, but one of the most valuable things you can do: log every species that moves into your yard as it establishes. Your observations become scientific data. Over time, your yard's species list becomes a measurable record of what native planting actually does for local biodiversity, and a pretty persuasive document for skeptical neighbors.

Consider joining or starting a Lafayette native yard project on iNaturalist to aggregate data across multiple yards.
